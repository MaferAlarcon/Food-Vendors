Food Vendors: Racism and Resilience in Bushwick

Mobile food vendors play a vital role in sustaining communities but face systemic barriers and unfair regulations.

June 2024

By: María Fernanda Alarcón

Bushwick’s streets are alive with the aromas and colors of mobile food vendors and immigrant-run businesses, offering affordable, culturally relevant goods. Despite their contributions, these vendors face heavy scrutiny, strict permitting limits, and fines that threaten their livelihoods. According to Marilyn Aguirre-Molina, Professor Emerita of Public Health at CUNY, the disparity is clear: “Media narratives often demonize immigrant communities instead of addressing inequitable systems like costly, limited permits.” A recent analysis shows Northwest Brooklyn has the largest concentration of mobile food vendors, with 613 operating in the area. Bushwick and Williamsburg follow with 433, underscoring their importance to these neighborhoods. While 1,000 veteran vendors operate legally, tens of thousands—non-veteran food and merchandise sellers—are forced into informal markets. These vendors endure stigmatization and penalties, even as they provide essential services and revitalize neighborhoods.
